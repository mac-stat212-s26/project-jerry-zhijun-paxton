Appendix A — Capital Without Category: Governance, Inequality, and the Micro-Enterprise Paradox in Brazil

Authors

Zhijun He

Jerry Miao

Paxton Boyd

Published

March 25, 202

Project Description

Brazil’s official firm-size classification (Simples Nacional)(https://thebrazilbusiness.com/article/taxation-regime-simples-nacional) sorts registered companies into micro, small, and medium/large tiers based on annual gross revenue. But revenue is a poor proxy for capital endowment. A sole proprietor earning 360,000 BRL per year is legally a “micro-enterprise” regardless of whether they hold 10,000 BRL or 10,000,000 BRL in declared capital stock. This creates a classification paradox: the same regulatory label encompasses both subsistence operations and well-capitalized investment vehicles.

This project uses Brazil’s CNPJ business registry(Cadastro Nacional da Pessoa Jurídica – National Registry of Legal Entities)(https://dados.gov.br/dados/conjuntos-dados/cadastro-nacional-da-pessoa-juridica—cnpj) — a near-census of all registered firms — to expose the internal heterogeneity that aggregate size categories conceal. We focus on two orthogonal dimensions that revenue-based classification ignores: within-tier capital inequality and governance structure (the legal role of the controlling owner). Our central argument is that “micro-enterprise” is not a coherent economic category but a regulatory label that shelters firms with vastly different capital endowments under the same umbrella — with direct implications for how development policy should target capital support.

Research Questions

  1. Within-tier inequality: Are micro-enterprises themselves internally unequal in capital endowments, or does the category represent a relatively flat distribution? How does within-tier inequality (measured by Gini coefficient) compare across size classifications?

  2. Governance as a capital predictor: Does owner qualification — Managing Partner, Beneficial Owner, or Sole Entrepreneur — predict capital accumulation independently of firm size? Does a governance premium persist even within the same regulatory tier, and if so, by how much?

  3. The “capital-rich micro”: Among micro-enterprises with unusually high declared capital stock, what combinations of legal form and governance archetype distinguish them from the capital-poor majority? What does this imply for how development programs should identify genuinely capital-constrained firms?

Dataset

Brazilian Companies (CNPJ)

The dataset is drawn from Brazil’s Cadastro Nacional da Pessoa Jurídica (CNPJ), the federal business registry administered by the Receita Federal (Federal Revenue Service). It contains registration records for firms across Brazil with the following key variables:

The dataset is publicly available via the TidyTuesday GitHub repository and requires no restricted access or IRB approval | Variable | Type | Description | |—|—|—| | capital_social | Numeric (BRL) | Declared capital stock at registration | | porte_empresa | Categorical | Firm size tier under Simples Nacional (Micro, Small, Medium/Large) | | natureza_juridica | Categorical | Legal form (LLC, sole proprietorship, EIRELI, etc.) | | owner_qualification | Categorical | Legal role of controlling owner (Managing Partner, Beneficial Owner, Sole Entrepreneur, Other) | | municipio | Categorical | Municipality of registration | | cnae_fiscal | Categorical | Primary sector code (CNAE classification) |

The working subset used in this project contains 141,332 observations after standard cleaning (removal of records with missing or zero capital stock, harmonization of legal-form codes). Capital stock is right-skewed and will be log-transformed for distributional analysis. The owner_qualification field is recoded into four governance archetypes: Managing Partner / Administrator, Sole Entrepreneur, Beneficial Owner (passive/investor), and Other.

Inspiration

This project grew out of a prior TidyTuesday analysis that identified a macro-level capital paradox: micro-enterprises constitute the majority of registered Brazilian firms yet control a disproportionately small share of total capital stock. That finding raises a harder question — is this gap primarily between size tiers, within them, or both simultaneously?

The answer matters for development policy. If the capital deficit is concentrated among a genuine subset of micro-enterprises while others are actually well-capitalized holding-company subsidiaries registered as “micro” because their revenue falls below the Simples Nacional threshold, then blanket micro-enterprise subsidies are a blunt and potentially misdirected instrument. This project is motivated by the broader development economics question of when categorical targeting works and when it fails — a question with relevance well beyond Brazil, extending to informal economy support programs across Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and Latin America.
